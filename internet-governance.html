Internet Governance

Explore how the internet is managed, who makes the rules, and how policy decisions affect your online experience.

5 lessons • 25 min read • Beginner • Updated 21 July 2026
Lesson 1 of 5 · Governance 20%

No single government or company owns the internet, yet it works the same way almost everywhere in the world — the same addresses, the same protocols, the same basic rules. That consistency doesn't happen by accident. It's the result of a system called internet governance: a mix of international organisations, national regulators, technical bodies, and civil society groups that shape how the internet is run.

Understanding this system matters because decisions made in these rooms — often far from Uganda — directly affect what you can access, how fast your connection is, and what happens to your data.

Key takeaway

Internet governance isn't run by one authority. It's shared between technical bodies that keep the network running, governments that regulate access within their borders, and companies that build the services on top.

Who actually manages the internet?

A handful of organisations handle the technical backbone that makes the internet function as one connected network rather than isolated islands.

  • ICANN — coordinates the domain name system and IP address allocation globally.
  • Internet Engineering Task Force (IETF) — develops the technical standards and protocols that let different networks communicate.
  • Regional Internet Registries, like AFRINIC for Africa — manage IP address allocation for the continent.
  • National regulators, like the Uganda Communications Commission (UCC) — license telecom operators and regulate access within Uganda.

The multi-stakeholder model

Most internet governance decisions follow what's called a "multi-stakeholder" approach — rather than governments alone setting the rules, decisions are shaped through dialogue between governments, the private sector, technical experts, and civil society, including ordinary users.

The Internet Governance Forum (IGF), held annually under the UN, brings these groups together specifically because no single actor can run the internet alone.

Where Uganda fits in

Uganda participates in this system at several levels: the UCC regulates local operators and sets rules on things like SIM registration and spectrum use, Uganda has its own national Internet Governance Forum (UIGF) to discuss local priorities, and Ugandan civil society organisations regularly engage in regional and global governance conversations on behalf of local users.

Did you know?
  • Uganda's first internet exchange point (IXP) allows local traffic to stay within the country, making local websites faster and cheaper to access.
  • The UCC has the authority to direct telecom operators to restrict access to specific services during declared emergencies.
  • Civil society groups can and do submit formal input to bodies like ICANN and the IGF on behalf of African internet users.

Why this affects you directly

Governance decisions show up in daily life more than most people realise: the cost of data bundles, whether a platform is available during elections, how quickly a new undersea cable improves your speeds, or whether a new law requires apps to store Ugandan user data locally.

  • Network shutdowns or throttling are typically directed through national regulators.
  • Data localisation rules affect where your information is stored and under whose laws.
  • Technical standards decided years in advance determine what's even possible on your device today.
Key takeaway

You don't need to sit in an ICANN meeting to have a stake in internet governance — every policy decided in these forums eventually reaches your phone, your data bundle, and your access to information.
