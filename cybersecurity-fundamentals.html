Cybersecurity Fundamentals

Understand common cyber threats, how to protect yourself from attacks, and best practices for staying safe online.

9 min read • Beginner • Updated 21 July 2026
Lesson 1 of 4 · Security 25%

Most young Ugandans experience the internet through a single device — a smartphone that holds their mobile money, their social media, their schoolwork, and their conversations. That makes it a valuable target. Understanding the most common cyber threats, and building a few simple habits, is the difference between an attack that fails and one that costs you your savings or your accounts.

This lesson introduces the threats you're most likely to encounter and the defences that actually work against them.

Key takeaway

Most successful cyberattacks don't rely on breaking through advanced technology — they rely on tricking a person into clicking, sharing, or approving something they shouldn't.

The threats you're most likely to face

Cybersecurity threats come in many forms, but a small number account for almost everything ordinary users run into.

Phishing

Phishing is a message — an SMS, email, or WhatsApp text — designed to look like it's from a bank, mobile money provider, or employer, asking you to click a link or share a PIN. It's currently the most common way accounts get compromised in Uganda, often through fake "your account has been suspended" or "you've won" messages.

SIM swap fraud

An attacker convinces your telecom provider to issue a new SIM card linked to your number, using stolen personal details. Once they control your number, they can intercept the one-time codes used to reset your mobile money or banking passwords.

Malware

Malicious software, often disguised as a free app, game, or "APK" file shared outside the official app store, that installs itself on your device and quietly steals data, tracks your activity, or locks your files until you pay a ransom.

Social engineering

Rather than hacking a device, the attacker manipulates a person — for example, calling and impersonating IT support or a relative in an emergency to pressure someone into sharing a password or sending money.

If a message creates urgency — "act now or lose access" — that urgency is usually the attack, not a genuine deadline.

Defences that actually work

  • Use strong, unique passwords — a different password for every important account, ideally stored in a password manager rather than memorised or reused.
  • Turn on two-factor authentication (2FA) — wherever it's offered, so a stolen password alone isn't enough to get in.
  • Verify before you click — check the sender's actual number or email address, not just the display name, before tapping any link.
  • Only install apps from official stores — Google Play or the Apple App Store, never a shared APK file from a message.
  • Be cautious on public Wi-Fi — avoid mobile money or banking transactions on open networks at cafés or transport hubs.
Did you know?
  • No legitimate bank, telecom, or mobile money provider will ever ask you to share your PIN or one-time password over the phone.
  • Enabling 2FA can block the vast majority of account takeover attempts, even if your password is stolen.
  • Uganda's Computer Misuse Act criminalises unauthorised access to computer systems and data, giving legal backing to cybercrime cases.

What to do if you've been targeted

  • Change the affected password immediately, from a different, trusted device if possible.
  • Contact your bank, mobile money provider, or telecom to freeze the account or SIM.
  • Report the incident to the Uganda Police Force's Cyber Crimes Unit or your service provider's fraud line.
  • Warn contacts if the attacker may have accessed your accounts to message people pretending to be you.
Key takeaway

Good cybersecurity isn't about knowing everything — it's about building a few consistent habits: unique passwords, 2FA, and a healthy pause before clicking anything urgent.
