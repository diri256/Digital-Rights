Artificial Intelligence & Policy

Learn how AI systems work, the ethical concerns around them, and what regulations are being developed in East Africa.

4 lessons • 20 min read • Advanced • Updated 21 July 2026
Lesson 1 of 4 · AI Policy 25%

Artificial intelligence already sits behind more of your daily life than it might seem — the system that recommends what you see on social media, the model banks use to decide who gets a loan, the chatbot answering customer queries. As these systems spread across East Africa, governments and regulators are starting to ask a harder question: who is accountable when an AI system gets it wrong, or causes harm?

This lesson covers the basics of how AI systems work, the main ethical concerns they raise, and where regulation currently stands in the region.

Key takeaway

AI regulation in East Africa is still forming. Understanding the underlying concerns — bias, transparency, and accountability — will matter more than memorising any specific rule, since the rules are actively changing.

How AI systems actually work — the short version

Most modern AI systems, including the large language models behind chatbots, work by learning patterns from huge amounts of existing data, then using those patterns to generate predictions or new content. They don't "understand" the world the way a person does — they generate the statistically likely next answer based on what they were trained on.

  • Training data — the examples a system learns from, which shape everything it can and can't do well.
  • Model — the underlying system that turns input into output based on patterns learned during training.
  • Bias — when a system reflects unfair patterns present in its training data, often affecting some groups more than others.

The main ethical concerns

Most policy debates around AI come back to a handful of recurring concerns:

Bias and discrimination

If a system is trained mostly on data from one region, language, or demographic, it can perform worse — or unfairly — for people outside that group. This has shown up globally in areas like facial recognition and hiring tools.

Transparency

Many AI systems function as a "black box" — even their developers can't fully explain why a specific output was produced, which makes it hard to challenge a decision that affects you, such as a loan denial.

Accountability

When an AI-assisted decision causes harm, it's often unclear whether responsibility sits with the developer, the company deploying it, or the regulator that approved its use.

A recurring principle across most draft AI policies is "meaningful human oversight" — the idea that a person, not just an algorithm, should remain accountable for significant decisions.

Where regulation stands in East Africa

No East African country has a single comprehensive AI law yet, but movement is underway at both national and regional levels.

  • Uganda has been developing a national AI policy framework, building on existing data protection law as a foundation.
  • Kenya has published draft national AI strategy documents addressing governance, skills, and infrastructure.
  • The African Union adopted a continental AI strategy aimed at coordinating AI governance across member states.
Did you know?
  • Existing data protection laws, like Uganda's Data Protection and Privacy Act, already apply to many AI systems that process personal data — even without AI-specific legislation.
  • The African Union's Continental AI Strategy calls for harmonised approaches to AI governance across the continent, rather than each country regulating in isolation.
  • Several regional tech policy organisations, including in Uganda and Kenya, actively consult on draft AI frameworks before they're finalised.
Key takeaway

You don't need to be a developer to have a stake in AI policy — as a user of AI-powered services, questions of bias, transparency, and accountability affect you directly, and regional frameworks are still being shaped.
