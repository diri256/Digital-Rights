Understanding Uganda's Data Protection and Privacy Act

What the law actually says about your personal information, who has to follow it, and the rights it gives you as a young person online.

8 min read • Beginner • Updated 14 July 2026
Lesson 2 of 5 · Privacy & Data 40%

Every time you register a SIM card, sign up for a boda-boda app, or create a social media account, you hand over personal information — your name, your phone number, sometimes your fingerprints. In Uganda, what happens to that information after you hand it over isn't left to chance. It's governed by a specific law: the Data Protection and Privacy Act, 2019.

This lesson breaks down what the Act actually protects, who it applies to, and — most importantly — what you can do if an organisation misuses your data.

Key takeaway

The Data Protection and Privacy Act gives you the right to know what personal data an organisation holds about you, to ask them to correct it, and in many cases to ask them to delete it entirely.

What counts as "personal data"?

The Act defines personal data broadly. It's not just your name and National ID number — it includes anything that can identify you, either on its own or combined with other details.

  • Your phone number, email address, and home address
  • Biometric data, like fingerprints or facial scans
  • Your location data from apps and mobile networks
  • Financial information, including mobile money transaction history
  • Photos or videos that clearly show who you are

Who has to follow the Act?

The law applies to any "data collector, data processor, or data controller" operating in Uganda — a deliberately wide net. In practice, this covers telecom companies, banks and mobile money providers, schools and universities, hospitals, government agencies like NIRA, and most apps and websites that ask you to create an account.

An organisation that collects your data must tell you why it's collecting it, get your permission where required, and keep it secure — the Act calls this the duty to process data "lawfully."

The main rights the Act gives you

Sections 24–27 of the Act translate into a handful of practical rights you can actually use:

  • Right to access — ask an organisation what data they hold about you.
  • Right to correction — ask them to fix inaccurate or outdated data.
  • Right to object — refuse processing for direct marketing purposes.
  • Right to erasure — ask for your data to be deleted when it's no longer needed.
Did you know?
  • The Act established the National Personal Data Protection Office (NPDPO) to enforce it and receive complaints.
  • Organisations that collect data must register with the NPDPO before they start processing personal data.
  • Consent given by a person under 18 for certain data uses may require a parent or guardian, depending on the context.

What can you do if your data is misused?

If you believe a company or institution has mishandled your personal data — shared it without permission, failed to secure it, or refused a legitimate correction request — you can lodge a written complaint directly with the NPDPO. You don't need a lawyer to do this, and the process is designed to be accessible to ordinary citizens.

For serious breaches, the Act also allows for fines and, in some cases, criminal penalties against the organisation responsible.

Practical steps you can take today

  • Read the privacy policy before you tap "I agree" on a new app.
  • Check what permissions an app requests — does a torch app really need your contacts?
  • Keep a record of which platforms you've registered your national ID or SIM with.
  • Know that you can request your data be deleted from services you no longer use.
Key takeaway

You don't have to accept every data request an app or company makes. Understanding your rights under the Act is the first step to using them.
