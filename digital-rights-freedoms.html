Digital Rights & Freedoms

Understand your fundamental digital rights — freedom of expression, access to information, and assembly online.

5 lessons • 25 min read • Beginner • Updated 21 July 2026
Lesson 1 of 5 · Rights 20%

The rights you're entitled to offline — to speak freely, to access information, to gather with others — don't disappear when you go online. They apply just as much on a phone screen as they do in a public square. This lesson covers what those digital rights actually mean in practice, and where they come from under Ugandan law.

Key takeaway

Digital rights aren't a separate category of rights invented for the internet — they're your existing constitutional freedoms, applied to an online environment.

Where these rights come from

Uganda's Constitution is the starting point. Article 29 guarantees freedom of speech and expression, freedom of the press and other media, and freedom of assembly and association — protections that courts and advocates increasingly apply to online spaces as well.

  • Freedom of expression — the right to share opinions, including on social media, without arbitrary restriction.
  • Access to information — the right to seek and receive information, reinforced by the Access to Information Act, 2005.
  • Freedom of assembly and association — the right to organise and coordinate with others, including through online platforms.

Where these rights get tested

In practice, digital rights are most often tested during moments of political tension — elections, protests, or public controversy — when the tension between free expression and public order becomes most visible.

Courts have repeatedly had to weigh legitimate public order concerns against constitutional protections for speech and assembly, both offline and online.

Laws that intersect with digital rights

  • The Computer Misuse Act, 2011 (as amended) — criminalises certain online conduct, including some forms of hate speech and misuse of computer systems, and has been the subject of ongoing debate over how broadly it's applied.
  • The Data Protection and Privacy Act, 2019 — protects personal data, which intersects with expression when it comes to what platforms may collect or share about users.
  • The Access to Information Act, 2005 — gives citizens a legal route to request information from public bodies.
Did you know?
  • Uganda's Constitutional Court has heard multiple cases weighing free expression against provisions of the Computer Misuse Act.
  • Ugandan civil society organisations, including the Collaboration on International ICT Policy (CIPESA), actively track and report on digital rights issues across the region.
  • Freedom of assembly protections have been interpreted by courts to extend to organising activity conducted primarily online.

Exercising your digital rights responsibly

Having a right doesn't mean every use of it is automatically without consequence — expression rights, for instance, don't cover defamation or incitement to violence. Understanding both the protections and their limits helps you engage online with confidence rather than uncertainty.

Key takeaway

Your constitutional rights to expression, information, and assembly extend online — but like any right, they exist alongside responsibilities and legal limits worth understanding.
