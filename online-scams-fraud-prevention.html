Online Scams & Fraud Prevention

Identify common online scams in Uganda, from phishing to SIM swap fraud, and learn how to protect yourself.

5 lessons • 25 min read • Beginner • Updated 21 July 2026
Lesson 1 of 5 · Security 20%

Scams targeting Ugandan mobile users have grown alongside mobile money and smartphone adoption — and they keep evolving. Most, though, follow a small number of recognisable patterns. Learning to spot those patterns is far more useful than trying to memorise every individual scam, since new ones appear constantly.

Key takeaway

Almost every scam relies on the same two ingredients: urgency and trust. Slow down and verify independently, and most scams lose their power immediately.

Common scams in Uganda today

Mobile money scams

A message claims you've received mobile money by mistake and asks you to send it back — except no money was ever sent, and the "confirmation" message was faked. Always check your actual mobile money balance and transaction history directly in the app, not through a forwarded message.

Fake job offers

Scammers advertise attractive jobs, often abroad, then ask for an upfront "processing" or "visa" fee before any real interview takes place. Legitimate employers do not typically ask candidates to pay money before being hired.

Phishing messages

Fake texts or emails impersonating a bank, telecom, or government agency, asking you to click a link and enter your PIN or password to "verify" your account or avoid suspension.

SIM swap fraud

An attacker gathers enough of your personal details to convince your telecom provider to issue a replacement SIM in your name, then uses it to intercept the one-time codes needed to access your mobile money or bank account.

Romance and relationship scams

A scammer builds an online relationship over weeks or months, often through social media or dating apps, before eventually asking for money to cover a fabricated emergency.

Fraud investigators consistently note that scams work by manufacturing urgency — a limited-time offer, a supposed emergency, or a countdown to account suspension — to stop victims from pausing to verify.

How to protect yourself

  • Verify independently — call your bank or provider back using the number printed on your card or their official website, not a number given in the suspicious message.
  • Never share your PIN or OTP — no legitimate organisation will ever ask for it over a call or message.
  • Slow down under pressure — urgency is the scam's engine; a genuine deadline can survive you calling to confirm it.
  • Check for small inconsistencies — misspelled sender names, unusual phone numbers, or a slightly-off company logo.
  • Set up a SIM swap PIN with your telecom provider where available, to make unauthorised swaps harder.
Did you know?
  • Uganda's Computer Misuse Act criminalises electronic fraud, giving victims a legal route to report and pursue cases.
  • Mobile money providers in Uganda typically offer dedicated fraud-reporting shortcuts or hotlines separate from general customer care.
  • Scam messages often use urgency language like "act within 24 hours" specifically because it reduces the chance you'll stop to verify.

If you've already been scammed

  • Contact your mobile money provider or bank immediately to attempt to freeze or reverse the transaction.
  • Change any passwords or PINs that may have been exposed.
  • Report the incident to the Uganda Police Force's Cyber Crimes Unit.
  • Warn friends or family if the scam may have used your identity to target them too.
Key takeaway

Falling for a scam is not a sign of carelessness — these schemes are specifically engineered to work on careful people under pressure. What matters most is acting quickly once you recognise it.
